# Functional Strength Programme

**Profile:** Male, 27, 103kg, 5'11"  
**Goal:** Joint health, flexibility, functional strength  
**Schedule:** 3 days/week — Tuesday, Wednesday, Friday  
**Session length:** 45–60 minutes (including warm-up)  
**Post-LASIK consideration:** No breath-holding under load (Valsalva). Exhale on every effort.

| Exercise | Reps / Duration | Purpose |
|----------|----------------|---------|
| World's Greatest Stretch | 5 per side | Hip, thoracic, and ankle mobility in one movement |
| Hip 90/90 Transitions | 5 per side | Internal and external hip rotation |
| Band Pull-Aparts | 15 reps | Rear delt activation, scapular health |
| Goblet Squat Hold | 30 seconds | Deep squat mobility, ankle dorsiflexion |

> **Why this warm-up matters:** Training first thing in the morning means spinal discs are fully hydrated and more vulnerable to compression. A proper warm-up raises core temperature and prepares the spine for loading. Dynamic mobility is more effective than static stretching before resistance training.

## Research Backing

### Programme Design

- A 2025 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* found that **resistance training through full range of motion improves joint flexibility comparably to dedicated stretching programmes** (Favro et al., 2025). This validates training through full ROM on every exercise rather than needing separate flexibility sessions.
- A systematic review of high-intensity functional training (HIFT) found **significant positive effects on strength, power, speed, endurance, and agility** across 19 studies involving 911 healthy individuals (BMC Public Health, 2025).
- Hybrid programmes combining resistance training with dynamic mobility produce **greater joint health outcomes than isolated stretching alone**.

### Exercise Selection — Evidence

**Trap Bar Deadlift**
- Produces higher force output, velocity, and power compared to conventional barbell deadlifts (Swinton et al., 2011).
- Reduces lumbar spine loading due to a more upright torso position, shifting demand to the quadriceps while still training the full posterior chain.
- Easier to learn and safer for non-powerlifters. Recommended as a primary lower-body movement by Greg Nuckols (Stronger by Science) and Dr. John Rusin.

**Bulgarian Split Squat**
- EMG research shows greater gluteus maximus recruitment compared to bilateral back squats (Mackey et al., 2021, Georgia Southern University).
- Unilateral training corrects left-right imbalances, engages stabiliser muscles neglected in bilateral movements, and reduces spinal compression compared to loaded back squats.
- Recommended in ACL rehabilitation protocols due to favourable hamstring-to-quadriceps co-activation ratios (Mausehund et al., 2018).

**Turkish Get-Up**
- EMG analysis shows it challenges the shoulder stabilisers (rotator cuff, deltoids, trapezius) through multiple planes of motion while simultaneously training core anti-rotation and hip mobility (Howarth et al., 2019, published in *Journal of Bodywork and Movement Therapies*).
- Used clinically for shoulder rehabilitation — Gray Cook has documented its application in rotator cuff injury recovery.
- The ACE (American Council on Exercise) rates the TGU as one of the highest-activating core exercises across all four major abdominal muscles tested.

**Farmer Carries**
- A 2024 study found farmer carries produce greater bilateral activation of the longissimus, multifidus, rectus abdominis, and external obliques compared to static hold exercises (PMC, 2024).
- Grip strength is classified as a biomarker of aging, correlating strongly with mortality risk and overall health in large epidemiological studies.
- Dr. Stuart McGill's research shows loaded carries — particularly asymmetric variations — uniquely challenge the lateral core musculature (obliques, quadratus lumborum) in ways no other exercise replicates.

**Kettlebell Swings**
- McGill and Marshall (2012) found kettlebell swings create rapid muscle activation-relaxation cycles reaching ~50% MVC for low back extensors and ~80% MVC for gluteal muscles, making them highly effective for posterior chain power development.
- The unique posterior shear loading pattern (opposite polarity to traditional lifts) may explain why many individuals report improved back health from regular swing training.
- Lake and Lauder (2012) found kettlebell swings produce power output comparable to jump squats, with strong carryover to athletic performance.

**Romanian Deadlift (Slow Eccentric)**
- Eccentric loading at long muscle lengths promotes addition of sarcomeres in series, effectively lengthening muscle fibres — this is protective against hamstring strains during explosive movements.
- A cohort study of track athletes found single-leg RDLs performed during warm-up reduced mild-to-moderate hamstring injury risk by 66% (Otani et al., 2025, *International Journal of Sports Physical Therapy*).
- The RDL simultaneously builds strength and hamstring flexibility through the eccentric loading phase, eliminating the need for separate hamstring stretching.

**Pallof Press**
- Anti-rotation core training (resisting movement rather than creating it) more closely reflects how the core functions in real life and sport — stabilising the spine during limb movement.
- Superior to traditional flexion-based core exercises (crunches, sit-ups) for developing functional trunk stiffness.

### LASIK Safety

- Post-LASIK, most patients can return to full exercise routines within one month of surgery with no long-term restrictions.
- The primary ongoing consideration is avoiding the Valsalva manoeuvre (breath-holding under maximal strain), which spikes intraocular pressure. This programme uses rep ranges of 5–15, which keep intensity well below maximal grinding territory.
- Controlled breathing (exhale on exertion) on every rep mitigates any IOP concerns.

## Contradictions, Limitations & Honest Trade-Offs

This section documents the known criticisms and contrary evidence so you can make informed decisions.

### 1. Trap Bar Deadlift — Less Posterior Chain Than Conventional

**The criticism:** The trap bar shifts load towards the quads and away from the hamstrings and lower back compared to a conventional barbell deadlift. Research by Camara et al. found higher biceps femoris (hamstring) and erector spinae activation with the conventional deadlift. If maximal posterior chain development is the sole goal, the conventional deadlift is arguably superior.

**Why it's kept:** Your programme already addresses this gap. The RDL on Friday specifically targets the hamstrings through eccentric loading, and the kettlebell swings train hip extension power. The trap bar was chosen for its lower spinal loading, easier technique, and higher power output — all of which align with your health-first goals. The combination of trap bar + RDL + swings covers the full posterior chain more thoroughly than a conventional deadlift alone would.

### 2. Turkish Get-Up — Contested Value

**The criticism:** Men's Health fitness director Ebenezer Samuel and others have argued the TGU is overrated because it provides "inconsistent loading" — you move through so many positions that no single muscle group gets optimally challenged. The time cost is high (~90 seconds per rep), and the complexity means you can't load it heavy enough to drive serious strength adaptation. Some coaches, including Dan John himself at older ages, have raised safety concerns about heavy TGUs and the risk of dropping a kettlebell on yourself.

**Why it's kept (with modification):** For your goals — shoulder stability, joint health, full-body coordination — the TGU is well-supported by clinical research (Howarth et al., 2019; ACE, 2025). It's programmed at light-to-moderate weight (2 × 3/side) specifically as a movement quality tool, not a strength builder. It's now placed first in the session when stabiliser muscles are fresh. If you find it doesn't add value after 8 weeks, replace it with a kettlebell windmill + half-kneeling overhead press combination, which targets the same patterns in a simpler format.

### 3. Frequency — Each Muscle Group Only Hit ~1.5× Per Week

**The criticism:** Schoenfeld's 2016 meta-analysis found training muscle groups twice per week produces superior hypertrophy compared to once per week. In the original version of this programme, some patterns (squatting, pulling) only had direct exposure once per week.

**How it's addressed:** The warm-up frequency top-ups (light pull-ups on Tuesday, light goblet squats on Wednesday) now give every major pattern at least two exposures per week. Additionally, Schoenfeld's later 2018 meta-analysis found that when total weekly volume is equated, frequency differences largely disappear. For strength and health goals (vs. maximum hypertrophy), once to twice per week is sufficient.

### 4. Friday — Double Hinge Day (Swings + RDL)

**The criticism:** Both kettlebell swings and Romanian deadlifts are hip-hinge dominant movements. Programming both on the same day could theoretically overtax the posterior chain and lower back, particularly for someone at 103kg returning to training.

**Why it works:** The two exercises serve different purposes — swings are ballistic/power (fast concentric, minimal eccentric) while the RDL is slow eccentric strength. EMG research by Zebis et al. (2013) shows both exercises preferentially target the semitendinosus over the biceps femoris, making them complementary rather than redundant. The swings warm up the hinge pattern, and the RDL deepens it under control. The total volume (4 × 12 swings + 3 × 8 RDL) is moderate. However, if your lower back feels excessively fatigued, swap the RDL to Tuesday (replacing the Pallof press position) and move the Pallof press to Friday. This is a legitimate individual adjustment.

### 5. No Direct Quad-Dominant Squat

**The criticism:** There's no barbell back squat or front squat. Some coaches argue that for maximal lower body strength and bone density, a heavy bilateral squat is irreplaceable.

**Why it's structured this way:** The trap bar deadlift on low handles produces significant quad activation (research shows higher vastus lateralis EMG than conventional deadlifts). The Bulgarian split squat provides unilateral quad work with reduced spinal compression. Together, they cover quad development while being more joint-friendly than a heavy back squat. If you want to add a squat later, the front squat would be the best fit — it reinforces an upright torso and has less spinal compression than the back squat.

### 6. Box Jumps With Plantar Fasciitis History

**The criticism:** Programming box jumps for someone with plantar fasciitis risk is questionable, even with the "step down" modification. Some physiotherapists would avoid them entirely.

**How it's managed:** The swap option (med ball slams) is built in from day one. Start with box jumps at a conservative height, step down every rep, and monitor symptoms. If any foot pain emerges in the first two weeks, switch permanently to med ball slams. No ego on this one — the power development benefit is identical with slams.

## Progression Guidelines

| Week | Approach |
|------|----------|
| Weeks 1–2 | Use conservative weights. Focus on movement quality and learning the warm-up flow. |
| Weeks 3–4 | Begin adding weight where all prescribed reps are completed with good form. |
| Weeks 5–8 | Progressive overload — small increases (2.5kg upper body, 5kg lower body) when you complete all sets cleanly. |
| Week 8–12 | Reassess. If strength is the priority, shift main lifts to lower rep ranges (3–5). If conditioning matters more, add density (shorter rest periods). |

**Deload:** Every 4th week, reduce all weights by ~40% and focus on movement quality. This supports joint recovery and long-term sustainability.

---

## Plantar Fasciitis Management

- **Box jumps:** Step down from the box rather than jumping down. This halves the impact on your feet.
- **If feet flare up:** Swap box jumps for med ball slams (same power output, zero foot impact). Swap sled pushes for battle ropes.
- **Daily foot care:** Roll a frozen water bottle under each foot for 5 minutes in the evening. Calf raises (slow eccentric) 3 × 15 daily can strengthen the plantar fascia over time.

## Summary

This programme is built around three principles:

1. **Full range of motion on every exercise** — research shows this builds flexibility as effectively as dedicated stretching.
2. **Functional, multi-joint movements** — trap bar deadlifts, carries, get-ups, and swings train the body as an integrated system, not isolated muscles.
3. **Sustainability** — 3 days per week, 45–60 minutes, with built-in deloads and swap options. Health is the goal, not burnout.

Train consistently, breathe on every rep, and progress gradually. Reassess at 12 weeks.
